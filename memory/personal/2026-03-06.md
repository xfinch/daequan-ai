## Developing the Proposal Strategy and Pricing

The client’s requested scope includes:
- Dedicated Internet (fiber/DI) with interest in 200, 300, 500, and 1000 Mbps options (client asked for 500 and 1 Gig; Susan had suggested 300).
- One BBE (Business Voice Edge) phone line, with potential for an additional basic seat in the back office.
- An additional TV box at $11.95.

Pricing and payback fundamentals:
- Jedi is the absolute price floor for DI—lowest permissible sell price—constrained by construction payback.
- Construction/payback is validated with the IRR tool; targets typically green above roughly 18% IRR (15% is rarely approved and risky if construction moves).
- Even with very low construction estimates (e.g., $7,000), proposals must meet payback thresholds. Given the preliminary figure, meeting payback at or near Jedi appears very likely across tiers, including 200 Mbps, 500 Mbps, and even 50 Mbps scenarios.
- Term length materially affects payback and Jedi by term (e.g., 60 vs. 36 vs. 24 months): longer terms improve IRR.

Specific price references discussed:
- 500 Mbps DI Jedi: $700.05/month (equipment separate). A 10% uplift would be $770.05, but the team recommends anchoring psychologically at $790 instead of quoting into the $800s for the same minimal gain.
- 1 Gig DI: $975/month on a 60-month term; $1,075/month on a 36-month term.
- Equipment fee: $39.95/month quoted separately for perception benefits (avoid bundling this into the base DI price).

Strategic pricing posture:
- Avoid arbitrary 20% uplifts over Jedi that risk sticker shock; when competition or sensitivity is expected, quote at Jedi or marginally above (e.g., $10 above).
- If construction later comes in higher, a small MRC cushion may prevent requoting; however, large uplifts can cost the deal (e.g., “liquor store guy” lost due to overpricing).
- Tiered option ordering: always present the highest bandwidth/price first to steer selection upward.
- Consider promoting small deltas between tiers so higher-tier adoption feels “worth it” (anchoring).
- DI deals can optionally include three free months’ credit in some cases.

Voice and equipment details:

Terminology and legal sensitivity:

## Structuring the Proposal Email and Defining Next Steps

Agreed working email framework:
- Introduction/connection: brief gratitude and context.
- Information delivery: concise, scannable presentation of tiered DI options (200/300/500/1000), with equipment fee stated once below the tiered table; list BBE seat, Edgemarc, installation, and the optional TV box at $11.95.
- Call to action: explicitly request time-bound availability to meet and decide how to proceed, e.g., “Do you have time on Monday or Tuesday for a quick meeting to decide how you want to move forward?”

Execution tactics:
- Keep the front matter concise—avoid duplicating heavy detail above the pricing block when the prices are clearly listed below.
- Quote DI base prices without bundling the $39.95 equipment fee; then add the equipment fee as a separate, universal line to maintain base-price attractiveness (e.g., “$980 plus $39.95” lands more favorably than “$1,020”).
- Position the highest bandwidth/price option at the top to improve MRC outcomes.
- Mention term sensitivity (“60-month pricing; 36-month is typically $50 more”) to set expectations and provide choice.
- Label the proposal and pricing as “preliminary” pending final survey results, despite high confidence on payback at/near Jedi.

Process and cadence:
- Proposal emails should ideally be drafted and delivered within hours of the client meeting while enthusiasm is high; the manager notes that, even with complexity, this should be a ~15-minute task once templates/snippets are in place.
- Immediately propose a review/signing meeting to reduce latency and avoid email-driven delays. Stats shared: stand-alone DocuSign yields ~50% completion; live signing calls close “almost every time.”
- Talk track for signing meetings: emphasize customer convenience, clarity, and that the rep will be present to explain any confusing contract language.

Enablement:
- The manager uses pre-written templates/signatures and Salesforce snippets to accelerate assembly (especially for coax). These approaches can be extended to DI and refined to include autopay/e‑bill and standard incentive structures. Xavier welcomes exposure to these systems to reduce ambiguity and speed time to proposal.
